C-BOM

Cryptographic Bill of Materials — the inventory and analysis of cryptographic algorithms, protocols, and implementations that protect systems and data.

What is a Cryptographic Bill of Materials?

A Cryptographic Bill of Materials (C-BOM) is a comprehensive inventory of all cryptographic algorithms, protocols, key management practices, and implementations used across a system or organization. It answers critical questions: What cryptographic primitives protect our data? Which algorithms are in use? What are their security properties? When do they expire?

Unlike generic software BOMs, C-BOM focuses specifically on the cryptographic foundations of security. It recognizes that cryptographic assumptions have lifetimes. Algorithms weaken. Keys degrade. Protocols become obsolete. A C-BOM tracks these temporal dependencies and identifies when migration or replacement is needed.

The core insight is critical: you cannot manage what you cannot measure. Without a clear inventory of cryptographic usage, organizations cannot plan for algorithm transitions, assess quantum readiness, or make informed decisions about long-term data protection.

What Goes Into a C-BOM?

A complete C-BOM documents:

  • Symmetric algorithms — AES, ChaCha20, DES (legacy), and other bulk encryption methods
  • Asymmetric algorithms — RSA, ECDSA, ECC, and their key sizes and curves
  • Hash functions — SHA-256, SHA-3, MD5 (legacy), and their applications
  • Key derivation functions — PBKDF2, Argon2, bcrypt for password hashing
  • Protocols and standards — TLS versions, HTTPS configurations, VPN implementations
  • Key management infrastructure — How keys are generated, stored, rotated, and retired
  • Implementation details — Libraries used (OpenSSL, BoringSSL, etc.), versions, and known issues
  • Data classifications — Which algorithms protect which types of data and for what duration
  • Regulatory compliance — Which algorithms meet FIPS, NSA, or industry-specific standards

This inventory should be discoverable, maintainable, and continuously updated as systems change.

Cryptographic Assumptions and Time Horizons

Every cryptographic algorithm relies on mathematical assumptions about computational difficulty. These assumptions are not eternal. They have time horizons.

Consider:

  • RSA-2048 — Believed secure today, but may face attack within 5-10 years if advances in factorization occur
  • SHA-1 — Deprecated due to collision vulnerabilities; should have been retired years ago
  • AES-128 — Secure for most near-term uses, but long-lived data may need AES-256
  • ECC curves — Some curves (P-256) face questions about NSA influence; others are preferred
  • Quantum risk — Current RSA and ECC provide zero post-quantum security; migration is essential

A C-BOM makes these temporal properties explicit: which algorithms are suitable for how long, and when replacement is needed.

Crypto-Agility and Algorithm Migration

A well-designed system does not lock itself into a single cryptographic algorithm forever. It should be crypto-agile: able to support multiple algorithms and transition between them as threats evolve.

Crypto-agility requires:

  • Algorithm abstraction — Cryptographic operations are abstracted so implementations can be swapped
  • Parallel support — New algorithms can be deployed alongside legacy ones during transition
  • Migration planning — Clear processes for deprecating old algorithms and adopting new ones
  • Testing and validation — New algorithms are thoroughly tested before being trusted with production data
  • Performance monitoring — Algorithm changes have performance implications that must be understood

Without crypto-agility, systems become brittle. When an algorithm must be abandoned (due to a break or quantum threat), rigid systems face catastrophic transition challenges.

Cryptographic Risk and Quantum Threats

The most pressing cryptographic threat is quantum computing. Quantum computers will break the mathematical assumptions behind RSA and ECC, rendering current asymmetric cryptography insecure.

This creates a critical inventory challenge: identifying all data and systems that depend on algorithms vulnerable to quantum attack.

  • Data with long confidentiality requirements — Medical records, state secrets, long-term contracts must be protected against harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks
  • TLS communications — HTTPS traffic encrypted today with RSA/ECC becomes vulnerable if quantum computers arrive before data expires
  • Digital signatures — Signatures created today may lose validity if the signing algorithm becomes quantum-broken
  • Key establishment — Perfect forward secrecy helps, but historical keys remain at risk

A C-BOM enables organizations to prioritize quantum-safe migration based on data sensitivity and exposure duration.

Legacy and Deprecated Cryptography

Many systems still use cryptographic algorithms that are known to be weak or deprecated. Understanding where legacy cryptography exists is essential for security.

  • MD5 — Cryptographically broken but still found in legacy systems and legacy protocols
  • DES — Completely insecure but embedded in legacy hardware, banking systems, and industrial control
  • SHA-1 — Vulnerable to collision attacks; deprecated by NIST but still widespread
  • RC4 — Broken cipher still found in legacy TLS and VPN implementations
  • 3DES — Weak by modern standards but used in PCI-DSS regulated systems

A C-BOM reveals where these weak algorithms hide, enabling targeted replacement efforts. However, some legacy systems may be irreplaceable, requiring compensating controls or isolation.

Cryptographic Supply Chain and Dependencies

Cryptographic implementations do not exist in isolation. They depend on:

  • Cryptographic libraries — OpenSSL, BoringSSL, NSS, libsodium; each with different security properties
  • Hardware security modules (HSMs) — Physical devices that generate and protect keys
  • Key management services — Cloud KMS, enterprise key vaults, or legacy on-premises solutions
  • Random number generators — Quality entropy sources are essential for cryptographic security
  • Standards and certifications — FIPS validation, Common Criteria, or industry-specific standards

Each dependency introduces risk. A C-BOM tracks these dependencies and identifies where single points of failure or vendor risks exist.

C-BOM as a Compliance and Risk Tool

C-BOM is increasingly required by regulatory frameworks:

  • NIST guidelines — Recommend cryptographic inventory as part of post-quantum cryptography preparation
  • PCI-DSS — Requires documented cryptographic protocols for payment card data
  • HIPAA — Mandates encryption standards for healthcare data
  • EU regulations — GDPR and upcoming quantum-safe requirements drive C-BOM adoption

Beyond compliance, C-BOM enables organizations to:

  • Plan quantum-safe transitions systematically
  • Identify and retire weak cryptography
  • Reduce cryptographic technical debt
  • Make informed architecture decisions about algorithm selection

C-BOM as a Living Inventory

A C-BOM is not a one-time artifact. It must be continuously maintained as:

  • New systems are deployed with new cryptographic choices
  • Algorithms are deprecated or broken, requiring replacement
  • Key rotation and retirement occur
  • Compliance requirements change
  • Quantum-safe algorithms mature and become deployable

Maintaining a living C-BOM requires:

  • Integration with development and deployment processes
  • Automated discovery of cryptographic usage
  • Regular audits and reviews
  • Clear ownership and accountability
  • Tools that track cryptographic inventory across hybrid environments

Organizations that maintain C-BOMs are better positioned to survive cryptographic transitions and adapt to emerging threats.

Component Relationships and Trust Networks

C-BOM is fundamentally about understanding relationships, not just inventories. The question is not "what components do we have?" but "how do these components depend on and trust each other?"

A trust network in a system includes:

  • Direct dependencies — Code that a system calls directly
  • Indirect dependencies — Code that dependencies call on behalf of your system
  • Implicit trust — Components that are assumed to be safe or trustworthy without active verification
  • Lateral trust — Components that do not directly depend on each other but share underlying infrastructure
  • Organizational trust — Teams and people who maintain or operate systems

The topology of this trust network determines how resilient the system is to failure, compromise, or degradation.

From Inventory to Understanding

Creating a C-BOM inventory (listing all components) is a start. But the real value comes from understanding:

  • Which components are critical?
  • What happens if each component fails?
  • Which components have single points of failure?
  • How would a failure cascade through the system?
  • Where are the trust boundaries?
  • Where is visibility lacking?
  • How do organizational constraints affect system security?

This understanding allows for better decision-making about risk management, mitigation, and survivability.

Uncertainty: Complete understanding of a complex system is not possible. Emergent behaviors appear at scale. Interdependencies are discovered incrementally. The map is never complete.

C-BOM as a Living Document

A C-BOM is not a one-time artifact. Systems change constantly. Dependencies are updated, removed, or added. New vulnerabilities are discovered. Vendors change. Organizations restructure.

A static C-BOM becomes obsolete immediately. A living C-BOM requires:

  • Continuous monitoring and updating
  • Regular review and re-assessment
  • Integration with development and operational practices
  • Visibility into changes and their implications
  • Documentation of why decisions were made

The effort required to maintain a true understanding of system dependencies is substantial and ongoing.

Note: Cryptographic Bill of Materials practice is still evolving. Standards for C-BOM format and content are emerging. Organizations are learning how to implement C-BOM discovery, management, and governance. The field is moving from research to operational practice.
